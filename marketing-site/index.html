What this is

Improv is how we work with Claude.

It covers what the bare model doesn't: planning against a brief, holding to a design system, adjusting a live page from the browser, and remembering decisions between sessions. We made it for ourselves, we use it daily, and it's a folder you can clone.

The toolkit

Three tools that earn their place. A foundation underneath.

These three are the center of the toolkit. Each owns a part of the loop - Sidecoach plans and designs, Justify validates and tunes in the browser, Beats records and recalls across sessions - and everything else in the package supports them.

The loop

Every build moves through the same loop.

Five steps, each one handing off to the next - and you stay in Claude and in the browser the whole way. Step through them below.

  1. 01

    Sidecoach turns a plain-language brief into a plan you approve before any code is written.

  2. 02

    The design pass runs against your own tokens and references, never a generic template.

  3. 03

    Claude Code writes working code straight into your own repo.

  4. 04

    Justify turns clicks on the live page into real, verified changes.

  5. 05

    Beats records the work as it happens and reads it back next session, on any machine.

The foundation

What holds the loop together.

A short Improv glossary: the deeper layer that does the quiet work. It isn't pitched separately - it's the substrate everything else runs on.

  • skills On-demand capabilities Claude pulls in when a task needs them: design orchestration, icon sourcing, motion patterns, memory analysis. They load when relevant and stay out of the way otherwise. See in reference ›
  • hooks Pre- and post-tool guards that enforce the rules at write time, not after: no skipped verification, no synthetic clicks during QA, no AI attribution in the output. See in reference ›
  • plugins Optional bridges. A browser pane (cmux) for visual checks, voice in and out, a Discord bot so you can reach Claude from your phone. Each one is opt-in. See in reference ›
  • reference A curated catalog of design references plus a single-page cheatsheet, so research starts from patterns that already work instead of a blank page. See in reference ›
  • tilt-lab A local workbench for shader and animated backgrounds. Audition an effect, tune it live, export it, and mount it behind a section. The hero on this page came out of it. See in reference ›

FAQ

Common questions.

What is this, exactly?

A toolkit for Claude Code - the tool Anthropic ships for working with Claude in your codebase. Improv adds focused tools on top of it for planning, designing, building, adjusting, and remembering work across sessions. It's a folder you clone, not a product you sign up for.

Do I need to know the terminal?

For the day-to-day loop, mostly no. Planning happens in plain language and adjustments happen by clicking on the live page in the browser. Someone does the one-time install at a terminal, but the work itself - describe it, watch it build, point at what to change - doesn't require the command line.

Does it replace my designer?

No. It enforces design decisions someone already made - the tokens, type, and rules in your DESIGN.md. It's faster at applying a system than inventing one. The taste still comes from a person; the tool just holds the work to it.

What does it cost, and does it phone home?

The tools are open source under the MIT license. There's no account, no dashboard, and no telemetry - nothing reports back. The only running costs are the model usage you already pay for through Claude Code, plus optional extras like voice, which bills against your own keys and is off unless you turn it on.

Can I adopt just one tool?

Yes. Every component installs on its own. Take Justify for in-browser adjustments and skip the rest, or run Beats for memory without the design stack. The installer breaks each piece out so you opt in component by component.

How does a team share memory?

Beats are plain files committed to your repo. When a teammate pulls the repo on their own machine, they get the same record of what was decided and done. Open Claude there and it reads the same history. The shared context rides along with the code, so there's no separate service to sync.

Is it safe to let it change the site?

It works on your local copy and the changes are ordinary commits you review like any other - nothing ships until you push. Guardrails block the genuinely destructive things, like force-pushes to main and production deploys, at the tool level. You watch each change land in the browser before it goes anywhere.
