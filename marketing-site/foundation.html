the rest of the stack / foundation

The quiet layer under the three tools.

Justify, Sidecoach, and Beats sit on a foundation of smaller pieces: the install, the on-demand skills, the guardrails, the bridges to your phone and browser, and a workbench for backgrounds. Most of it runs without you thinking about it. This is the map - for the full detail on any piece, the reference goes deeper.

Install

One command, then a picker.

Improv installs on a Mac with a single command. It downloads the toolkit, installs one shortcut called ampersand, and reloads your shell so the shortcut is live right away.

curl -fsSL get.improv.dev | bash

The picker.

Type ampersand and a checklist appears, every piece listed with a one-line description. Arrow keys to move, space to toggle, enter to confirm. Everything starts ticked, so if you want the whole set, just press enter. It is safe to run as many times as you like - re-running only touches what changed.

Pick just what you want.

If you already know, name it directly. ampersand --only sidecoach,memory installs a subset; --preset minimal takes a lean core; --dry-run prints what would happen without touching a file; ampersand --pull grabs the latest first. Come back to the shortcut any time to add a piece you skipped.

The skills

Specialists that step in when the work needs them.

Sidecoach is the front door for design work. Sitting beside it are a handful of specialist skills, each good at one kind of output. You do not memorize them or call them by name - you say what you want, and the right one steps in. Each reads your PRODUCT.md and DESIGN.md first, so what it makes matches your brand.

Platform-correct social assets.

When you want an asset built to a platform's exact spec - an Instagram carousel, a YouTube thumbnail, an Open Graph card. It knows the pixel dimensions, the safe zones a platform's own interface covers, and the smallest text that stays readable at thumbnail scale.

A multi-role design sprint.

When the task is large - a full landing page, a campaign whose pieces need to match. Instead of one straight pass, it runs a small team: research and copy first, builders in parallel on different sections, then a creative-director review that makes the sections hang together. For whole pages, not single components.

Generative backgrounds and real icons.

One skill ships real, tested effect source for animated and shader backdrops, tuned in tilt-lab. Another pulls icon artwork verbatim from an established library and holds a project to a single one, so a made-up, slightly-off icon never reaches your page.

Reflect.

When the Beats record grows large, reflect reads back across all of it at once to surface patterns, tensions, and gaps you might have missed. It is how a long-running project notices what it has been circling.

The bridges

Reach Claude from your browser, your voice, your phone.

A few optional pieces connect Claude to the rest of how you work. Each is opt-in, added from the picker or with --only.

The browser pane.

cmux is a split-pane terminal with a real browser built in. When Claude changes something visual, it opens the page in that pane, takes a screenshot, and looks at the result instead of guessing. It can click, type, and scroll the way a person would. The work you get back has been seen, not just compiled - a passing build is not proof a page looks right.

Talk to it, hear it back.

Hand Claude a voice memo and it transcribes the audio locally, no cloud and no key, treating the words as if you typed them. With the speaking side on, it reads a short summary of its replies aloud while keeping code on screen as text. Speaking uses OpenAI's text-to-speech, so it is the one piece that needs your own key; everything else, including listening, runs locally at no cost.

Claude in your pocket.

Pair a Discord bot once and message Claude from any device, including your phone, with replies coming straight back in the chat. Send a voice message and it transcribes the same way. Pairing is a deliberate, one-time, terminal-only step: Claude will never add a new person just because a Discord message asks it to.

tilt-lab

A local workbench for backgrounds.

When a hero or section wants a backdrop that moves, tilt-lab is where you watch it run and tune it before it ever lands in your page. It is driven from the design flow - Sidecoach delegates here whenever a backdrop is part of the work - so you reach it through the task rather than as a side trip.

Browse, layer, tune, export.

Browse a catalog of around twenty-five effects grouped by role, layer compatible ones into a stack and watch them composite in one live preview, tune each effect's parameters in real time with sliders, then export the finished stack as a single self-contained package. No build step, no account, no network round trip.

The preview is honest.

What you see in the workbench canvas is exactly what the exported component renders. The export mounts behind your section - underneath the content, matched to your design tokens, and aware of a reduced-motion preference so it falls still for anyone who has asked for less movement.

The guardrails

Checks you never have to think about.

As Claude works, a layer of quiet checks runs alongside it. You do not switch these on or call for them. Their whole job is to keep small, avoidable mistakes from reaching you - the kind that are easy to wave through in the moment and tedious to clean up later.

What they keep out of your work.

No emojis or em-dashes in anything that ships. Icons that come verbatim from a real library or do not ship at all. Generated interface code that points at your real DESIGN.md tokens instead of hard-coded colors. An interface that is opened in a real browser and looked at before the work is called done. Current model versions only, and never a commit signed in Claude's name. And every finished piece of work recorded as it happens, so nothing is lost if a session ends abruptly.

When a rule is wrong for the moment.

Now and then a guard gets in the way of legitimate work - you genuinely need to write the very thing it screens for. When that happens, Claude does not quietly route around the guard or soften your wording to slip past it. It stops and asks whether to allow it this once. The default is safe; the exception is explicit; you stay in control of the override.
